## The Concentration Problem

When you build on a platform, you are not just adopting a tool — you are accepting a governance relationship. The platform controls the API, the terms of service, the pricing, and the audience. Every feature you depend on can be changed, paywalled, or removed without your consent.

This is platform risk. It is the single most underestimated threat to digital sovereignty.

The pattern repeats across every layer of the stack: social media, cloud providers, app stores, payment gateways, operating systems. Each one creates value by reducing friction, and each one extracts rent by controlling the exit.

## The Cost of Convenience

Platforms are not malicious by default. They are optimised for their own incentives. Shareholder value, ad revenue, and lock-in are features, not bugs. When your interests diverge from the platform's — and they will — you discover that you were never the customer.

Consider the sequence:

- You invest time learning a proprietary tool.
- You build a presence on a closed network.
- You store data in a format only one vendor can read.
- You integrate a service that has no open alternative.

Each step is rational in isolation. Together they create irreversible dependency.

## What Reduces Risk

Open protocols are the structural answer. A protocol is not owned by anyone. Email, the web, RSS, Matrix, ActivityPub — these are substrates you can build on without asking permission. The cost is higher upfront. The exit cost stays low.

Practical steps:

- Prefer tools that export standard formats (Markdown, JSON, plain text).
- Host your own infrastructure where the marginal cost is reasonable.
- Maintain a presence on open protocols alongside any platform account.
- Audit your dependencies: what happens if this service shuts down tomorrow?

The goal is not to abandon platforms entirely. It is to ensure that no single platform can cut you off from your work, your audience, or your data.
