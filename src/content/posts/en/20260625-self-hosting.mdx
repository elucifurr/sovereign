## Beyond the Hobby Narrative

Self-hosting is often framed as a hobby for tinkerers. This framing serves the companies that would rather you pay them monthly for services you could run yourself. In reality, self-hosting is a structural decision about infrastructure control.

When you run your own email, your own file storage, your own analytics, your own communication tools, you change the power dynamic. You stop being a tenant on someone else's infrastructure and become the landlord of your own digital space.

The gap between self-hosted and managed services has narrowed significantly in the last decade. Tools like Docker Compose, Ansible, and container orchestration have commoditised what once required dedicated operations teams.

## What to Self-Host

Not everything needs to be self-hosted. The decision should follow a simple principle: host what is core to your autonomy, outsource what is commodity.

Start with:

- **Email.** Your email address is your digital identity. Owning your mail server means no provider can suspend your account or read your mail.
- **File storage.** Nextcloud or Syncthing gives you control over your documents without exposing them to a third party's cloud.
- **Analytics.** Self-hosted analytics (Plausible, Matomo) means your visitor data does not become a product for an ad network.
- **Communication.** Matrix or XMPP gives you private, interoperable messaging that no single company controls.

Each service you self-host adds operational overhead. The question is whether the autonomy gained justifies the cost.

## The Baseline

Start small. A $10 VPS can run email, a web server, and a Matrix instance. The skills you learn — DNS, TLS, backup strategies, monitoring — transfer directly to professional infrastructure work.

Self-hosting is not about doing everything yourself. It is about having the option to do what matters, and knowing the difference.
