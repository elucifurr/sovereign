## Protocols Are the Constitution

A platform is a service you use. A protocol is a system you participate in. The distinction is not semantic — it is the difference between renting space and owning the building.

Protocols define the rules of communication without defining the actors. Email works because SMTP, IMAP, and DKIM are open standards, not because a single company runs the email service. The web works because HTTP, HTML, and DNS are protocols. Anyone can implement them, extend them, or build on top of them without permission.

This is the most resilient architectural pattern we have for digital systems. It is also the most threatened.

## Protocol Erosion

The pattern of protocol erosion is well documented: an open protocol enables a service, a company centralises that service for convenience, and the protocol atrophies as users migrate to the centralised version.

RSS readers were replaced by platform algorithms. XMPP messaging gave way to proprietary silos. Even the web itself is being displaced by native apps and platform-specific formats.

Each time a protocol is replaced by a platform, autonomy decreases. You lose the ability to choose your client, control your data, and switch providers without friction.

## Defending the Protocol Layer

The defence is not nostalgia. It is deliberate architectural choice:

- Build on protocols, not just APIs. An API can be revoked. A protocol that others implement is harder to kill.
- Choose software that interoperates. A tool that speaks standard protocols is a tool you can replace.
- Support services that federate. Federated services (email, Matrix, the fediverse) distribute power across operators.
- Document the protocols you depend on. If your workflow relies on a proprietary integration, you have a single point of failure.

Protocols are not a technical detail. They are the constitutional layer of the digital world. Neglect them, and the platforms will draft the laws.
